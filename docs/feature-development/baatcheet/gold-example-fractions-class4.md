# Gold Example — Class 4 Fractions Tutoring Dialogue

**Source:** User-shared 2026-04-27 (AI-research-generated benchmark).
**Purpose:** Reference standard for Baatcheet Dialogue Quality V2. The shape we're aiming for. See `dialogue-quality-v2-designed-lesson.md`.

## Topic & rationale

**Topic:** Introduction to fractions — naming, equal parts, comparing 1/2 with 1/4.

Of all candidate Class-4 topics, fractions is the single richest for a Socratic dialogue. Densest set of well-documented misconceptions for this age group; maps perfectly onto Concrete-Pictorial-Abstract progression (fold paper → draw a bar → write 1/2); Indian everyday life is full of natural fraction moments — sharing roti, splitting a chocolate bar with a sibling, cutting a watermelon. Concrete hooks the student already lives with — Vygotsky's ZPD.

## Misconceptions woven in (intentional)

1. **Whole-number bias** — believing 1/4 > 1/2 because 4 > 2. Surfaces at Card 12. (Documented in ~65% of at-risk Grade-4 fraction-ordering errors — Malone & Fuchs, 2017.)
2. **Ignoring the "equal parts" rule** — thinking any 4 pieces of a roti can be called 1/4, even if pieces are different sizes. Surfaces at Card 24.
3. **Different-whole confusion** — thinking 1/2 of a big bar equals 1/2 of a small bar, since "half is half." Surfaces at Card 32.

## The conversation (40 cards)

**Card 1 – Tutor:** Hey! Quick question before we start. Do you have a brother or sister at home? When mom gives a chocolate bar to share, who gets more?

**Card 2 – Student:** I have a younger sister. We share. Sometimes she takes more and I get angry!

**Card 3 – Tutor:** Ha, I get it! Today we will learn a math idea that fixes this fight. It is called fractions. Have you heard that word before?

**Card 4 – Student:** I think so. My friend said something about half. Is half a fraction?

**Card 5 – Tutor:** Yes! Half is a fraction. Good thinking. Now tell me. You have one roti. You want to share with your sister equally. How will you cut it?

**Card 6 – Student:** I will cut it in the middle. Two pieces. One for me, one for her.

**Card 7 – Tutor:** Perfect. Now picture I draw that roti as a circle. I cut it down the middle. Each piece is called one half. We write it like this: 1/2. Cool?

**Card 8 – Student:** Okay. But why is there a line and two numbers?

**Card 9 – Tutor:** Great question. The bottom number tells us how many equal pieces we cut the roti into. The top number tells us how many of those pieces we are talking about. So 1/2 means: 2 equal pieces, take 1.

**Card 10 – Student:** Oh! So if I cut it into 4 pieces and take 1, it is 1/4?

**Card 11 – Tutor:** Wow, you got it fast! Yes, that is one fourth. Now picture this. Your sister gets 1/4 of the roti. You get 1/2. She starts crying. Why do you think she is crying?

**Card 12 – Student:** Hmm. Maybe she wanted more? But 4 is more than 2. So 1/4 should be more, right?

**Card 13 – Tutor:** Aha! Lots of kids think exactly that. Let us test it together. Take a piece of paper. Fold it once in the middle. How many pieces do you see when you open it?

**Card 14 – Student:** Two pieces. Same size.

**Card 15 – Tutor:** Good. Each piece is 1/2. Now fold the paper again. Open it. How many pieces now?

**Card 16 – Student:** Four pieces.

**Card 17 – Tutor:** Each one is 1/4. Now look. Put one 1/2 piece next to one 1/4 piece. Which one is bigger?

**Card 18 – Student:** Oh wait! The half is much bigger. The 1/4 is small.

**Card 19 – Tutor:** Yes! See what happened? When we cut into more pieces, each piece becomes smaller. So 1/2 is bigger than 1/4, even though 4 is a bigger number than 2.

**Card 20 – Student:** That is strange. The bigger number means smaller piece?

**Card 21 – Tutor:** Yes, when it is the bottom number. Think of it like this. One pizza. Share between 2 friends, each gets a big slice. Share between 10 friends, each gets a tiny slice. More friends, smaller slice.

**Card 22 – Student:** Oh! That makes sense. More people, less for each.

**Card 23 – Tutor:** Exactly. You just figured out something big. Now let me ask you a tricky one. I draw a roti cut into 4 pieces. But the pieces are not the same size. Some big, some small. Is one piece still 1/4?

**Card 24 – Student:** Umm. There are 4 pieces. So yes? It is 1/4?

**Card 25 – Tutor:** Let me ask you something. If your sister got the smallest piece and you got the biggest, would she be happy?

**Card 26 – Student:** No way. She will fight. It is not fair.

**Card 27 – Tutor:** Right. So for fractions to work, the pieces must be equal. Always. If pieces are not equal, we cannot call them 1/4. The word "equal" is the secret word in fractions.

**Card 28 – Student:** Okay. So 1/4 means 4 equal pieces, take 1. Got it.

**Card 29 – Tutor:** You said it perfectly. Let us try one together. I cut a sweet into 8 equal pieces. You eat 1 piece. What fraction did you eat?

**Card 30 – Student:** 8 on the bottom, 1 on top. So 1/8?

**Card 31 – Tutor:** Yes! High five. Now one more tricky thing. Your friend Aarav says, "I ate 1/2 of my chocolate." You say, "I ate 1/2 of mine too. So we ate the same amount." Is that always true?

**Card 32 – Student:** Yes? Half is half. Same thing.

**Card 33 – Tutor:** Hmm. What if Aarav had a big chocolate bar and you had a small one? Both of you ate half. Who ate more chocolate?

**Card 34 – Student:** Ohhh. Aarav ate more. His half was bigger because his bar was bigger.

**Card 35 – Tutor:** Yes! Brilliant thinking. So fractions are about pieces of the same whole. When the wholes are different, halves can be different too. Tricky, right?

**Card 36 – Student:** Yes. My head is spinning a little.

**Card 37 – Tutor:** That is okay. Spinning means your brain is growing. Quick check. I cut a watermelon into 6 equal pieces. You take 2. What fraction did you take?

**Card 38 – Student:** 6 on bottom, 2 on top. So 2/6.

**Card 39 – Tutor:** Spot on. Today you learned three big things. Fractions need equal pieces. Bigger bottom number means smaller piece. And the whole must be the same when we compare. You worked hard today.

**Card 40 – Student:** Thanks! Now I can share roti with my sister fairly. No more fighting.

## Design principles (post-script)

1. **Curiosity hook with a real-life sibling moment (Cards 1-2)** — opens not with a definition but with a fight the student has actually had. Instantly places the topic inside the student's lived experience. The student's "she takes more and I get angry" gives the tutor a thread to pull on for the rest of the lesson.

2. **Activate prior knowledge before teaching (Cards 3-6)** — the tutor asks what the student already knows about "half" and how they would cut a roti, instead of starting with the term "fraction." Rosenshine's first principle, foundation of Vygotsky's ZPD.

3. **Open question that deliberately surfaces the whole-number bias (Card 11)** — "Why is she crying?" is a hypothetical that invites the student to project a wrong reason, which lets the misconception come from the student's mouth, not the tutor's. AutoTutor's "expectation- and misconception-tailored" move.

4. **Resolving misconceptions with concrete experience, not correction (Cards 13-18)** — tutor never says "you are wrong." Instead, the student folds paper and sees the truth. Pure Concrete-Pictorial-Abstract: physical paper → mental picture → the symbols 1/2 and 1/4. Productive struggle plus a hands-on disproof is far more durable than being told.

5. **Funnel-to-focus questioning when the student is stuck (Cards 23-27)** — instead of explaining "equal parts," the tutor uses one focusing question ("Would your sister be happy?") that lets the student work out the rule themselves. The fairness frame is doing the cognitive work, not the formula.

6. **Worked example then quick check (Cards 29-30, 37-38)** — Rosenshine's "guided practice" then "independent practice." 8-piece sweet is the tutor-led worked example; the 6-piece watermelon is a clean check that the student can apply the rule without scaffolding.

7. **Process praise, not ability praise (Cards 19, 23, 35, 39)** — "you figured out," "brilliant thinking," "you worked hard today" — never "you are smart." Dweck's growth mindset language, framing learning as effort, not talent. Card 37's "spinning means your brain is growing" reframes confusion itself as progress.

8. **Three explicit takeaways at the close, not a vague summary (Card 39)** — tutor names the three rules the student now owns: equal pieces, bigger bottom = smaller piece, same whole when comparing. Each maps directly to one of the three misconceptions the lesson was designed around, making the learning goal visible and reviewable.

## Additional patterns (observed but not in original post-script)

- **Narrative spine threading.** Sister-fairness opens (Card 1-2), recurs at the first trap (Card 11), is reused as the funnel question (Card 25), and closes the dialogue (Card 40). Lifts conversation from sequence-of-explanations to one connected story.
- **Tiny beats.** Most cards are 1-3 sentences. Tutor cards stretch only at articulation moments (after the student has done the work). 40 cards average to ~one beat each.
- **Textured student voice.** "Hmm" (12), "Umm" (24), "Oh wait!" (18), "Ohhh" (34), "My head is spinning" (36). Sounds humans make, not scripted prompt-shaped openings.
- **Conversational tutor voice.** "Ha, I get it!" (3), "Wow, you got it fast!" (11), "Aha!" (13), "High five" (31), "Spot on" (39). No textbook sentences in 40 cards.
- **Student as a character with a life.** Younger sister, friend Aarav, gets angry over chocolate. Particulars established in opening cards, threaded through for character continuity.
- **Escalated analogies.** Pizza shared with 2 vs 10 friends (Card 21) consolidates after paper-folding evidence — extreme case after concrete proof. Sweet (8 pieces, worked) → watermelon (6 pieces, independent check).
