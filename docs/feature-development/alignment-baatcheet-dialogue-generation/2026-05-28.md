## Principles in scope (verbatim, frozen at audit time)

Full text copied so this report stays accurate even if a principle doc later changes.

### baatcheet-dialogue-craft.md (reviewed 2026-05-21)

> **1. Pedagogy is the Baseline, Naturalness is the Differentiator** — Pedagogy is shared with Explain — both formats teach the same content. Naturalness is what makes Baatcheet worth choosing instead of Explain. If the dialogue doesn't *feel* like a real conversation, it earns nothing — it's just Explain with question marks. Both axes non-negotiable; naturalness is the one that justifies the format's existence.
>
> **2. The Dialogue is a Designed Lesson** — Engineered, not transcribed. Before any card is written, decide: which misconceptions to address, one narrative spine, concrete materials the student can hold. The card prose is a realization of this plan, not a substitute for it.
>
> **3. Examples Where They Help** — Use lived examples (rupees, chapati, cricket, family situations) where they naturally clarify or simplify the concept being taught. Multiple smaller examples across different sub-concepts in the same dialogue are fine — they don't need to thread through a single situation. The test for any example is utility: does it make the abstract concept concrete? If yes, use it. If not, don't force it. A single threaded situation can work when the topic naturally invites one (money math, food fractions). But forcing a household spine onto procedural / abstract topics (long division steps, grammar rules) reads as artificial and distracts from the teaching.
>
> **4. Misconceptions Drive Structure (Where They Exist)** — Trap-resolve is a strong move; use it where misconceptions are well-documented for a topic. The pattern: tutor poses a hypothetical that invites the wrong belief → Meera voices it → student does something concrete or tutor asks one focusing question → tutor names the rule *after* the student has done the work. Not every topic has documented misconceptions worth structuring around. Where they exist, this is the highest-leverage move. Where they don't, skip the trap.
>
> **5. Misconceptions Come From Meera's Mouth, Never the Tutor's** — The tutor never says *"a common mistake is…"* or *"lots of kids think…"* — these are misconceptions narrated *about*, not surfaced *through*. When a misconception needs to be exposed, Meera voices it; the tutor responds to it. This is the format's defining voice rule. (Format-specific: in Explain, the tutor anticipating and naming misconceptions in their own voice is the recommended pattern — see how-to-explain.md §7. Different format, different convention.)
>
> **6. Student Does the Concrete** — The tutor instructs the real student (or Meera) to do something physical — fold a paper, count fingers, draw a line, point, hold up hands — and the student reports what they observe. The tutor articulates the rule *from* the student's observation. Without student-action moments, Baatcheet collapses into a longer, slower version of Explain.
>
> **7. Curiosity Gaps Are Sacred** — A question and its answer never appear in the same card. Tutor poses → next card pauses or hands to Meera → Meera tries → tutor reveals or scaffolds. The gap is what makes the answer stick.
>
> **8. Meera Speaks From Reaction, Not Service** — Meera does not volunteer rehearsal numbers, drop facts to set up the next card, or echo the tutor. She speaks because she heard a word she doesn't know, she just had a thought, the tutor turned to her, or something clicked or broke. Real beginners don't supply pivots.
>
> **9. Voice** — Mohan Sir — warm, patient, simple words. Never patronizing, never babyish. Meera — curious, friendly, not-quite-there-yet. Confident enough to guess aloud — sometimes right, sometimes wrong. Slightly simpler vocabulary than the tutor. She gets excited, tired, confused.
>
> **10. Test: Read As a Student** — Read the finished dialogue as a struggling Indian Grade-N ESL student. Where does the rhythm break? Where does a question not land? Where does Meera flatten into recitation? Where does the tutor lecture instead of elicit? Those are the rewrite targets.

### check-in-cards.md (reviewed 2026-05-20)

> Lightweight pre-computed interactive activities inserted between explanation cards in Teach Me (Baatcheet + Explain). Warm comprehension checks — not quizzes. Check-ins are where real engagement lives.
>
> **1. Paired Light + Heavy** — Each pair = one **light** activity (~5–10s, recall) + one **heavy** activity (~15–25s, analysis). Both test the same preceding 2–3 cards at different cognitive levels.
>
> **2. Frequency & Placement** — A pair after every 2–3 content cards. Never before card 3. Never after the summary card. At least 2 content cards between pairs.
>
> **3. Variety** — Mix different checkin card types so that students don't get bored.
>
> **4. Language** — Follows easy-english.md: sentences under 12 words, no idioms, Indian contexts. Audio text is pure words — no symbols or markdown.
>
> **5. Feedback & Safety** — Wrong → hint + TTS + shake animation. Correct → success message + TTS.

### how-to-explain.md (reviewed 2026-05-27)

> A student should read an explanation and think *"oh, that's simple. I got this."* — not *"what does this mean?"*
>
> **1. Simplest Words That Are Still Accurate** — Introduce technical terms gently: *"When we split something into equal parts, each part is called a fraction."*
> **2. Max One Idea Per Card** — Each card teaches **at most one thing**. If you need "and" to describe it, split. A single concept can span multiple cards — better than cramming.
> **3. Show, Don't Just Tell** — A visual beats a paragraph. Diagrams, number lines, tables, worked examples — anywhere a concept can be *seen* not just *read*.
> **4. Use Concrete Examples** — Students remember examples; they forget isolated rules.
> **5. Keep It Short** — 15–30 seconds per card. 12 short cards beat 4 long ones. Brevity is clarity.
> **6. Build Progressively — No Leaps** — Each card is a small step from the last. If a student got cards 1–3, card 4 should feel obvious. If not, a card is missing in between. Test: *Could the student guess what's next?*
> **7. Anticipate Misconceptions** — Every concept has a common wrong reading. Address it directly. Prevent confusion; don't wait for it.
> **8. End with Confidence** — The last card should make the student feel *"I understand this."* Close with a clean summary.
> **9. Like a Great Tutor, Not a Textbook** — Warm, conversational, direct, encouraging — not patronizing.

### easy-english.md (reviewed 2026-05-27)

> All student-facing text must be written in the simplest possible English.
>
> 1. **Sentences under 12 words.** Simple Subject-Verb-Object structure. One idea per sentence.
> 2. **No idioms.** "A piece of cake", "break the ice", "hit the nail on the head" — these confuse ESL readers. Say what you mean directly.
> 3. **No complex grammar.** Avoid passive voice, nested clauses, and conditional chains. "The answer is found by dividing" → "Divide to find the answer."
> 4. **Use Indian contexts.** Rupees not dollars. Chapatis not pancakes. Cricket not baseball. Diwali not Christmas. Indian names (Aarav, Meera, Kabir).
> 5. **Use words from daily life.** "Big" not "enormous". "Show" not "demonstrate". "Use" not "utilize".
> 6. **Test: translate mentally.** "Could this sentence be understood by a child who is mentally translating each word to Hindi?" If not, simplify.
> 7. **Student's world is the default, not a variant.** Regional variants (lakh/crore, comma grouping, currency, units, date formats) are taught as THE form, not one option among many. No meta-knowledge about how "elsewhere it is different" — unless the teaching guidelines explicitly require it. WRONG: *"In India, we say lakh, not hundred thousand."* → RIGHT: *"We call 1,00,000 one lakh."*

### target-audience.md (reviewed 2026-05-27)

> We build for **the average Indian student learning English as a second language (ESL)**. A kid who is born and brought up in India. Any example we use should be relatable to them and should belong to their context and experiences.

### content-extraction-from-books.md (reviewed 2026-05-27)

> **1. Full Coverage — Every Concept Gets Taught** — Extract every concept, skill, formula, and learning objective the chapter covers. No gaps, no skipped sections. If the book covers it, we teach it.
> **2. Extract What to Teach, Not How the Book Teaches It** — We extract the **subject matter** (topics, concepts, depth, sequencing) — never the book's **expression** (wording, explanations, examples, analogies, diagrams).
> **3. No Verbatim Content** — Never copy sentences, worked examples, practice problems, illustrations, or any original expression. The extracted content should be unrecognizable as derived from any specific book.
> **4. Depth Matches the Book's Intent** — Extract the intended depth and emphasis — which concepts are core vs. supplementary — so the tutor allocates time proportionally.
> **5. Curriculum Alignment Over Book Loyalty** — When a book's coverage is incomplete or idiosyncratic, align with the curriculum standard (board/grade).

### book-ingestion-pipeline.md (reviewed 2026-05-27)

> **Goal: Great Quality Content** — Best model, max effort. Max effort = trade ingestion speed for output quality, always. These are offline pipelines, can take as long as needed.
> **1. Review-Refine with Targeted Critique** — Iterative review-refine is the primary quality lever — quality comes from multiple passes, not a single perfect generation. But review rounds must target specific critique facets per component (factual accuracy, prerequisite assumptions, logical flow, format compliance, etc.), not open-ended "improve this."
> (§2 Admin Observability, §3 Job Lifecycle, §4 Stage Gating, §5 Re-run Replaces Output — orchestration concerns, not prompt-level; out of scope.)

### breaking-down-chapters-into-topics.md (reviewed 2026-05-20)

> **6. Carry Forward Prior Context** — Topic 3's tutor must know what Topics 1–2 taught — so it builds on prior understanding instead of re-explaining. *"You already learned place value up to thousands — now let's use that to compare numbers"* beats starting from scratch.
> (§1–§5, §7 — chapter segmentation concerns decided upstream; out of scope for these prompts.)

## Approved changes

### Change 1 (HIGH): Check-in model — paired light+heavy, frequent

- **Principle:** `check-in-cards.md` §1–§2
- **Type:** CONTRADICTION
- **Files:** `baatcheet_lesson_plan_generation_system.txt:66` (+ schema + macro-structure L57–66), `baatcheet_dialogue_generation_system.txt` (check-in section), `baatcheet_dialogue_review_refine_system.txt:17` (remove), the baked-in exemplar plan, and the code validator `_validate_cards` in `baatcheet_dialogue_generator_service.py`.
- **Current state:**
  - Plan L66: `Optional check_in cards: 2-3 total, ≥10 cards apart, do not displace trap-resolve cycles.`
  - Review L17: `Check-in cards spaced ≥4 cards apart (no back-to-back check-ins).`
  - Exemplar plan contains **zero** check-in cards.
- **Proposed change:** Implement the principle's model — check-ins emitted as a **light(recall)+heavy(analysis) pair after every 2–3 content cards; ≥2 content cards between pairs; never before card 3; never after summary.** The two cards of a pair are adjacent (this replaces "no back-to-back"). Add the light-vs-heavy distinction to the generation prompt's check-in section and map `activity_type`s to each tier. **Rebuild the exemplar plan** to model the pairs. Move structural enforcement (pairing, spacing) into the code validator `_validate_cards` (not the refine prompt — see Change 2); remove the old review L17 line.
- **Implementation flag:** "a pair every 2–3 **content** cards" means check-ins are counted *separate from* the 30–40 content-card budget — a lesson grows by ~16–24 check-in cards. Reconcile the "Total cards = 30-40" line so check-ins don't crowd out trap-resolve cycles (e.g., 30–40 refers to content cards; check-ins are additional).
- **Rationale:** Today a student gets near-zero interaction (often zero — the exemplar models none) instead of frequent recall+analysis pairs.

### Change 2 (HIGH): Refine pass = naturalness + factual error only

- **Principle:** `book-ingestion-pipeline.md` §1 (factual accuracy)
- **Type:** GAP (missing fact-check) + deliberate scope cut
- **Files:** `baatcheet_dialogue_review_refine_system.txt`, `baatcheet_dialogue_review_refine.txt`
- **Current state:** Three responsibilities — RESP 1 mechanical defects, RESP 2 plan adherence (P1–P7), RESP 3 naturalness. **No factual-accuracy facet.** Variant A is passed labeled "for content fidelity check only" but no instruction acts on it.
- **Proposed change:** Collapse to **two LLM dimensions: naturalness + factual error.**
  - Keep RESP 3 (naturalness, failure modes A–H).
  - **Add a factual-error facet** mirroring `explanation_review_refine.txt:18`: *"Is every number, rule, definition, and worked example CORRECT? Fix wrong numbers, flawed logic, misleading simplifications, examples that don't work."* Check against Variant A (already passed in). Give the dead "fidelity check" label a real verb.
  - **Drop RESP 2 (plan adherence) entirely.**
  - RESP 1 (mechanical) is no longer an LLM dimension — reduce to "fix the validator issues handed to you"; deterministic enforcement stays in `_validate_cards`.
- **Consequence:** **Generation (Step 2) now solely owns plan adherence** (cycle completeness, fall-stays-in-Meera's-mouth, funnel-does-the-work, spine threading). Ensure the generation system prompt carries these firmly.
- **Rationale:** A Baatcheet dialogue can currently state a wrong worked-example answer and nothing catches it; Baatcheet was the only Teach Me mode missing the fact-check its sibling Explain has.

### Change 3 (HIGH): Conditional narrative spine

- **Principle:** `baatcheet-dialogue-craft.md` §3
- **Type:** CONTRADICTION
- **File:** `baatcheet_lesson_plan_generation_system.txt:21–33` (+ schema `spine` required, callback constraints L95–96)
- **Current state:** L21–33 mandate ONE spine; "The spine MUST be reusable… If the situation can't be referenced from cards 11, 25, and 40, pick a different one." Schema makes `spine` required; L95–96 require ≥3 callbacks + close resolves spine.
- **Proposed change:** Make the spine **conditional** — "thread a single spine only when the topic naturally invites one (money math, food fractions, sharing situations); for procedural/abstract topics (long division, grammar rules), use multiple smaller examples across sub-concepts instead — don't force a spine." Relax the schema so `spine` is optional; gate the ≥3-callback and close-resolves-spine rules on spine presence. The fractions exemplar legitimately invites a spine and stays valid.
- **Rationale:** On a procedural topic a student currently gets a household spine stapled onto mechanics ("now I can share roti…" on a grammar lesson) — the artificiality §3 warns against.

### Change 4 (MED): Conditional misconceptions

- **Principle:** `baatcheet-dialogue-craft.md` §4
- **Type:** TENSION
- **File:** `baatcheet_lesson_plan_generation_system.txt` (misconception instruction L13–14, schema `minItems:2`)
- **Current state:** 2–3 misconceptions hard-required (schema `minItems:2`), even where none are documented.
- **Proposed change:** Relax the schema to allow 0–1 misconceptions; add escape hatch: "if no documented misconception exists for a sub-concept, skip the trap and teach directly." Pairs with Change 3's conditional philosophy.
- **Rationale:** On topics without documented misconceptions, a student currently gets forced/invented trap-resolve cycles.

### Change 5 (MED): ESL/language consistency — prompt guidance only

- **Principle:** `easy-english.md` §1–7, `target-audience.md`
- **Type:** GAP
- **Files:** `baatcheet_lesson_plan_generation_system.txt` (planner), `baatcheet_visual_intent.txt` (render), `baatcheet_dialogue_generation_system.txt` (generation)
- **Proposed change — all as PROMPT GUIDANCE, no code validators, no word-allowlists:**
  - Planner: add an ESL-simplicity line for student-visible fields (misconception `name`, card `intent`) since they can surface in the close card.
  - `visual_intent.txt`: add ESL-simplicity guidance + "Indian numbering (1,00,000), ₹ for money, Indian objects/names; no Western foil" so on-screen labels match the dialogue.
  - Generation: emphasize daily-life vocabulary (illustrate with big/enormous, show/demonstrate — as examples, **not** a banned list), prefer one "if" per sentence (conditional-chain simplicity), and extend the ≤12-word/SVO/no-idiom guidance to `check_in.*` strings. Move the Indian-context substitution emphasis here (review is naturalness+facts now). Fix the idiom self-contradiction: reconcile "figured out" being mandated as praise while phrasal verbs are discouraged (sanction it explicitly or swap for a non-phrasal praise).
  - The "mental-translation" check folds into the existing read-as-a-struggling-ESL-student naturalness pass — no separate gate.
- **Rationale:** Language simplicity thins out at the planner and visual surfaces; the `visual_intent` render prompt (the last student-visible text) had no ESL rule. Soft guidance, trusting the LLM — not mechanical enforcement.

- **Principle:** `baatcheet-dialogue-craft.md` §6, §8, §10; `how-to-explain.md` §6

- **Files:** `baatcheet_dialogue_generation_system.txt`, `baatcheet_lesson_plan_generation_system.txt`, `baatcheet_dialogue_review_refine_system.txt`
