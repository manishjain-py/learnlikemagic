# Principles: Scorecard & Progress Tracking

The scorecard exists to motivate the student first, reassure the parent second. (The system reads the same data to adapt — but that's not what the scorecard is *for*.) When these conflict, the student wins: our learner is the average Indian ESL kid, often not a top performer, and a scorecard is never neutral to them — it's either confidence or shame.

Parents are the paying customer and want detail. For now they see the student's scorecard; a parent-specific view is future work — not a reason to make the student's view colder.

## 1. Mirror Facts, Never Interpret

Show concrete, attributable facts — coverage and grades tied to specific work. Never add an opinion about the student: no AI-generated strengths, weaknesses, or trends. A practice score (even an LLM-graded free-text answer) is a fact about specific answers; "your weakness is fractions" is a judgment about the kid. First yes, second never. The reason is trust: we don't yet trust AI judgment about a student on a report card a parent reads. High bar — not a bolted door.

## 2. Honest Numbers, Warm Framing

Never hide the deficit; never let it wound. Real numbers, framed so a struggling kid feels forward motion, not failure. Celebrate effort, nudge toward practice. First job: keep the student wanting to continue.

## 3. Current State, Not History

The scorecard shows where the student is *now*. Coverage measured against the current plan, not a historical union. Practice shows the latest graded score — not best, not average. Plan changes can swing the numbers; we accept that. Honest about today beats flattering about yesterday.

## 4. Coverage Is a Teaching Record

Coverage counts only structured instruction that maps deterministically to plan concepts (today: Teach Me). Free-form modes (Clarify Doubts) don't count, however deep — crediting them needs an AI to judge what was "really covered," breaking §1. The exclusion is the price of coverage you can trust, not a favored feature. Once taught, a concept stays covered: accumulates across sessions, never resets.

## 5. Show Less, Not More

Every metric earns its place by serving the learner. Latest score does; a visible attempt count doesn't — "tried 7 times" demotivates, and the count serves the system, not the kid. Compute internally what you need; show only what helps the student.

## Future direction: retention

Coverage answers "what we taught," not "what you still remember." A future signal — time/review-based, never AI-guessed — will flag concepts going stale, without erasing the coverage record. Distinct from prerequisite refreshers, which run before a chapter and carry no scoring.
