# Principles: Breaking Down Chapters into Topics

How we decompose a textbook chapter into topics for AI tutoring. Goal: it should feel like a thoughtful tutor planned the chapter — not a machine that split pages into fragments.

## 1. The Chapter as a Journey

Topics together tell the chapter's story in a logical, progressive arc. No concept orphaned, repeated, or out of place.

## 2. Cap Topics, Not Floor Them

5–20 minute lessons, depending on complexity — what a student would call "today's lesson." Most chapters land at 5–7 topics; 3 is fine for short chapters. No minimum. Hard rule: don't go so granular the list feels endless.

## 3. Student-Centered Boundaries

A boundary falls where a student naturally takes a break and feels done. In doubt: *would a good tutor stop here and say "great, we'll continue tomorrow"?*

## 4. Group by Concept, Not Section

Skills serving the same big idea belong in one topic, even when the textbook splits them across pages. Group by learning outcome, not page breaks.

E.g., a textbook may put *"addition of 2-digit numbers without carrying"* on one page and *"addition of 2-digit numbers with carrying"* on the next — both belong in one topic: *adding 2-digit numbers.*

## 5. Meta-Skills Aren't Topics

Meta-skills like practice or revision don't need their own topic — the app's practice features cover them.

## 6. Carry Forward Prior Context

Topic 3's tutor must know what Topics 1–2 taught — so it builds on prior understanding instead of re-explaining. *"You already learned place value up to thousands — now let's use that to compare numbers"* beats starting from scratch.

## 7. Plan the Chapter, Then Extract Pages

See the whole chapter first, then decide how to divide it — not 3 pages at a time hoping the pieces fit. Plan the skeleton from the table of contents, then fill in details per topic.
