# Principles: Breaking Down Chapters into Topics

These principles guide how we decompose a textbook chapter into topics for AI tutoring. The goal is a learning experience that feels like a thoughtful tutor planned the whole chapter — not a machine that split pages into fragments.

## 1. Complete the Chapter as a Journey

Topics should collectively tell the story of the chapter. A student who finishes all topics should have covered the entire chapter in a logical, progressive arc: **introduce → build → apply → explore → review.** No concept should be orphaned, repeated, or out of place.

## 2. 5-7 Topics per Chapter

A manageable number that doesn't overwhelm students. Each topic should be a substantial 20-40 minute lesson — something a student would naturally call "today's lesson." If a chapter has 13 topics, the student sees an endless list and loses motivation before starting.

## 3. Student-Centered Boundaries

A topic boundary should fall where a student would naturally take a break and feel a sense of completion. "I learned how to compare 4-digit numbers" feels complete. "I learned to compare the first digit" doesn't. When in doubt, ask: *would a good human tutor stop here and say "great, we'll continue tomorrow"?*

## 4. Conceptual Grouping Over Textbook Sectioning

Related skills that serve the same big idea belong in one topic, even if the textbook presents them across separate sections or pages. "Skip counting 4-digit numbers" and "expanded form of 4-digit numbers" both serve the same idea: *understanding how to represent 4-digit numbers.* Group by learning outcome, not by page breaks.

## 5. Each Topic Carries Context of What Came Before

The tutor for Topic 3 must know what was taught in Topics 1-2. This prevents re-explaining concepts and lets the tutor build on prior understanding naturally. "You already learned about place value up to thousands — now let's use that to compare numbers" is vastly better than starting from scratch every time.

## 6. No Page Overlap Between Topics

Each page should belong to exactly one topic. If two topics claim the same page range, the boundaries weren't properly identified. Overlapping pages mean overlapping content, which means the student hears the same thing twice.

## 7. Meta-Skills Fold into Practice

Strategies like "Does my answer make sense?" or "estimation as a check" are not standalone topics. They should be woven into the practice and application of other topics. A separate topic for a meta-skill feels hollow to a student — there's nothing concrete to practice.

## 8. Chapter-Level Planning Before Page-Level Extraction

Good topic structure requires seeing the whole chapter first, then deciding how to divide it — not processing 3 pages at a time and hoping the pieces assemble well. Plan the topic skeleton from the chapter's table of contents and overall structure, then fill in details per topic.
