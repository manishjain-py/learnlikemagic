# Principles: Baatcheet Dialogue Craft

**Purpose:** An alternative delivery format to Explain — same teaching, rendered as a scripted dialogue between Mohan Sir (tutor) and Meera (peer learner). Built for students who find one-way explanation harder to stay engaged with.

How we author conversational teaching dialogues for Baatcheet.

Pairs with `how-to-explain.md`, `easy-english.md`, `check-in-cards.md`.

---

## 1. Pedagogy is the Baseline, Naturalness is the Differentiator

Pedagogy is shared with Explain — both formats teach the same content. Naturalness is what makes Baatcheet worth choosing instead of Explain. If the dialogue doesn't *feel* like a real conversation, it earns nothing — it's just Explain with question marks. Both axes non-negotiable; naturalness is the one that justifies the format's existence.

## 2. The Dialogue is a Designed Lesson

Engineered, not transcribed. Before any card is written, decide: which misconceptions to address, one narrative spine, concrete materials the student can hold. The card prose is a realization of this plan, not a substitute for it.

## 3. Narrative Spine

One real Indian-household situation threads through the entire dialogue. Not a soft example — *the spine*. Opening plants it, middle calls back to it, close resolves it.

Examples: a fight with a younger sibling over chocolate; counting Diwali money from grandma; the population number on a road-trip milestone; sharing roti at dinner.

Without a spine, a long exchange is disconnected mini-explanations. The spine gives the conversation memory and the student one thread to hang every concept on.

## 4. Misconceptions Drive Structure (Where They Exist)

Trap-resolve is a strong move; use it where misconceptions are well-documented for a topic. The pattern: tutor poses a hypothetical that invites the wrong belief → Meera voices it → student does something concrete or tutor asks one focusing question → tutor names the rule *after* the student has done the work.

Not every topic has documented misconceptions worth structuring around. Where they exist, this is the highest-leverage move. Where they don't, skip the trap.

## 5. Misconceptions Come From Meera's Mouth, Never the Tutor's

The tutor never says *"a common mistake is…"* or *"lots of kids think…"* — these are misconceptions narrated *about*, not surfaced *through*. When a misconception needs to be exposed, Meera voices it; the tutor responds to it. This is the format's defining voice rule.

(Format-specific: in Explain, the tutor anticipating and naming misconceptions in their own voice is the recommended pattern — see `how-to-explain.md` §7. Different format, different convention.)

## 6. Student Does the Concrete

The tutor instructs the real student (or Meera) to do something physical — fold a paper, count fingers, draw a line, point, hold up hands — and the student reports what they observe. The tutor articulates the rule *from* the student's observation.

Without student-action moments, Baatcheet collapses into a longer, slower version of Explain.

## 7. Curiosity Gaps Are Sacred

A question and its answer never appear in the same card. Tutor poses → next card pauses or hands to Meera → Meera tries → tutor reveals or scaffolds. The gap is what makes the answer stick.

## 8. Meera Speaks From Reaction, Not Service

Meera does not volunteer rehearsal numbers, drop facts to set up the next card, or echo the tutor. She speaks because she heard a word she doesn't know, she just had a thought, the tutor turned to her, or something clicked or broke. Real beginners don't supply pivots.

## 9. Voice

**Mohan Sir** — warm, patient, simple words. Never patronizing, never babyish.

**Meera** — curious, friendly, not-quite-there-yet. Confident enough to guess aloud — sometimes right, sometimes wrong. Slightly simpler vocabulary than the tutor. She has a body and a face — gets excited, tired, confused.

## 10. Character Particulars

Meera has a life. Particulars chosen at the start of the dialogue — a younger sibling, friend Aarav, dadi's tin of ₹100 notes — thread through for continuity. Generic Meera is forgettable; particular Meera is a person the student remembers. Abstract traits ("Meera is curious") are vibes, not particulars.

## 11. Emotional Reframing

Acknowledge the student's emotion in the dialogue. *"My head is spinning a little"* → tutor: *"That is okay. Spinning means your brain is growing."* Confusion-as-progress.

## 12. Process Praise, Not Ability Praise

*"You figured that out."* / *"Brilliant thinking."* / *"You worked hard today."* — never *"You're smart."* Praise the work, not the trait.

## 13. Test: Read As a Student

Read the finished dialogue as a struggling Indian Grade-N ESL student. Where does the rhythm break? Where does a question not land? Where does Meera flatten into recitation? Where does the tutor lecture instead of elicit? Those are the rewrite targets.
