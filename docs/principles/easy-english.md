# Principle: Easy English for Indian ESL Students

Our students are Indian children for whom English is NOT their first language. They think in Hindi or their regional mother tongue and read English as a second language. All student-facing text must be written in the simplest possible English.

## Rules

1. **Sentences under 12 words.** Simple Subject-Verb-Object structure. One idea per sentence.
2. **No idioms.** "A piece of cake", "break the ice", "hit the nail on the head" — these confuse ESL readers. Say what you mean directly.
3. **No phrasal verbs.** "Figure out" → "find". "Come up with" → "think of". "Work out" → "solve". Use the single-word equivalent.
4. **No complex grammar.** Avoid passive voice, nested clauses, and conditional chains. "The answer is found by dividing" → "Divide to find the answer."
5. **Use Indian contexts.** Rupees not dollars. Chapatis not pancakes. Cricket not baseball. Diwali not Christmas. Indian names (Aarav, Meera, Kabir). References should feel familiar to an Indian child.
6. **Use words from daily life.** Prefer words a child hears at home and school. "Big" not "enormous". "Show" not "demonstrate". "Use" not "utilize".
7. **Test: translate mentally.** Before finalizing any text, ask: "Could this sentence be understood by a child who is mentally translating each word to Hindi?" If not, simplify.
8. **Student's world is the default, not a variant.** The student lives in India — this IS their reality, not a flavor of something else. Never label their context as "the Indian way" or compare it to "the Western way / American style / international format." When a concept itself has a regional variant (number names like lakh/crore, comma grouping, currency, units, date/time formats), teach the student's form as THE form — not as one option among many. A child does not need meta-knowledge that "elsewhere it is different."
   - WRONG: *"In India, we say lakh, not hundred thousand."* → RIGHT: *"We call 1,00,000 one lakh."*
   - WRONG: *"523,476 uses the Western style. Indian style is 5,23,476."* → RIGHT: *"5,23,476 is correct — commas go after 3 digits from the right, then every 2."*
   - **Exception:** if the textbook/curriculum itself teaches the comparison (e.g., a geography topic on number systems across countries), follow the source.
