# Research: Check-In Card Formats

Research into interactive micro-activity formats for comprehension check-ins between explanation cards. Goal: deeper thinking, higher engagement, mobile-friendly, 5-30 seconds each.

## Current Formats (6)

| Format | Bloom's | Time | What it tests |
|---|---|---|---|
| pick_one | Remember (L1) | ~5s | Quick recall, definitions |
| true_false | Remember (L1) | ~5s | Common misconceptions, rule testing |
| fill_blank | Understand (L2) | ~10s | Applying a rule just taught |
| match_pairs | Understand (L2) | ~20s | Vocabulary, related definitions |
| sort_buckets | Analyze (L4) | ~15s | Classification into 2 groups |
| sequence | Apply (L3) | ~15s | Ordering, steps, number sequences |

Observation: most current formats are recognition-based (L1-L2). sort_buckets is the most engaging because it requires analysis. Students actively want more check-ins, especially the deeper ones.

## Research Sources

- **EdTech platforms**: Duolingo (tap-the-pairs, word bank, match madness), Kahoot (puzzle, slider, drop pin), Brilliant (interactive manipulation, puzzle-first pedagogy), Quizlet (match, gravity), DragonBox (digital manipulatives, sandbox), IXL (handwriting recognition, adaptive difficulty), SplashLearn (in-game manipulatives)
- **Learning science**: Desirable Difficulties (Bjork), Generative Learning (Fiorella & Mayer), Predict-Observe-Explain, Productive Failure (Kapur), Confidence-Based Assessment
- **Assessment standards**: PARCC/SBAC technology-enhanced item types

## Key Insight from Learning Science

Formats that require **generation over selection** produce stronger retention:
- **Desirable Difficulties** (Bjork): harder-feeling practice = stronger long-term retention
- **Generative Learning** (Fiorella & Mayer): constructing, explaining, or predicting beats selecting
- **Predict-Observe-Explain**: predicting before seeing the answer surfaces misconceptions far better than post-testing
- **Productive Failure** (Kapur): brief struggle before the answer deepens understanding

## Proposed New Formats

### Tier 1 -- High impact, implement first

#### spot_the_error
Show a worked solution or statement with a deliberate mistake. Student taps the wrong step/part.

- **Bloom's**: Analyze (L4) -- must understand the correct process to find deviations
- **Time**: 10-20s
- **Mobile**: Tap on the erroneous step/line
- **Best for**: Math, grammar, science explanations
- **Example** (place value): "347 = 300 + 40 + 8. Tap the mistake." Student taps "8" (should be 7).
- **Why**: Detective feeling. Error analysis is one of the strongest learning strategies for building deep understanding. Students must evaluate each step against their own knowledge.

#### odd_one_out
Show 3-4 items. Student taps the one that doesn't belong.

- **Bloom's**: Analyze (L4) -- requires finding the shared property among distractors
- **Time**: 5-15s
- **Mobile**: Simple tap
- **Best for**: Math (number properties), science (classification), vocabulary
- **Example** (place value): "Which doesn't belong? 450, 320, 37, 510." Student taps 37 (only 2-digit number).
- **Why**: Puzzle-like feel. Forces categorization thinking. Very fast, high engagement.

#### predict_then_reveal
Present a scenario, ask "What do you think will happen?", student picks prediction, then sees actual answer with explanation.

- **Bloom's**: Apply/Analyze (L3-L4) -- activates prior knowledge, surfaces misconceptions
- **Time**: 15-25s
- **Mobile**: Tap prediction choice, then read reveal card
- **Best for**: Math (estimation), science (experiments), any concept with surprising results
- **Example** (place value): "If you add 1 to 999, how many digits will the answer have?" Options: 3/4/5 digits. Reveal: "4 digits! 999 + 1 = 1,000."
- **Why**: Backed by Predict-Observe-Explain research. Curiosity-driven. Wrong predictions create strong "aha" moments that stick.

#### swipe_classify
Cards appear one at a time. Student swipes right for Category A, left for Category B. Tinder-style binary sort.

- **Bloom's**: Analyze (L4) -- rapid classification decisions
- **Time**: 15-30s for 5-8 cards
- **Mobile**: Swipe gesture -- extremely natural on phones
- **Best for**: All subjects. Binary classifications: even/odd, living/nonliving, fact/opinion
- **Example** (place value): Swipe right if the number has a 0 in the tens place, left if not. Cards: 305 (right), 472 (left), 109 (right), 831 (left).
- **Why**: Same depth as sort_buckets but faster, more fun. The swipe gesture is inherently satisfying for kids on phones.

#### estimation_slider
Student drags a slider to estimate a numerical answer. Partial credit based on proximity.

- **Bloom's**: Apply (L3) -- requires number sense and approximate reasoning
- **Time**: 5-15s
- **Mobile**: Drag slider thumb -- natural mobile gesture
- **Best for**: Math (number sense, measurement, fractions), science (quantities)
- **Example** (place value): "About how many hundreds are in 2,847?" Slider range 0-50. Correct: 28.
- **Why**: Novel interaction unlike any current format. Builds number sense. Partial credit rewards close answers instead of binary right/wrong.

### Tier 2 -- Strong, implement next

#### two_truths_one_lie
Show 3 statements. Two are true, one is false. Student taps the false one.

- **Bloom's**: Evaluate (L5) -- must judge truthfulness of each statement independently
- **Time**: 10-20s
- **Mobile**: Tap one of 3 cards
- **Best for**: All subjects. Great for surfacing misconceptions.
- **Example** (place value): "A) In 4,562 the digit 5 is in the hundreds place. B) 3,000 + 400 + 20 + 1 = 3,421. C) The value of 7 in 1,732 is 70." Student taps C (value is 700).
- **Why**: Familiar social game format. Evaluating multiple statements is harder than judging one (true_false).

#### build_the_answer
Provide number/word tiles. Student drags them into slots to construct the correct answer.

- **Bloom's**: Apply/Create (L3-L6) -- constructing rather than selecting
- **Time**: 10-25s
- **Mobile**: Drag tiles into slots
- **Best for**: Math (equations, expressions), grammar (sentence construction)
- **Example** (place value): Tiles: [3][0][0][4][0][+][+][7]. "Build the expanded form of 347." Student constructs: 300 + 40 + 7.
- **Why**: Generative learning research shows constructing answers produces deeper retention than selecting from options.

#### would_you_rather
Present two options, student picks one, then sees which is actually correct/better and why.

- **Bloom's**: Evaluate (L5) -- weighing options and reasoning
- **Time**: 10-20s
- **Mobile**: Tap one of two large buttons
- **Best for**: Math (number sense, estimation), critical thinking
- **Example** (place value): "Would you rather have 5 hundreds or 48 tens?" Reveal: "5 hundreds = 500, but 48 tens = 480. So 5 hundreds is more!"
- **Why**: Inherently engaging choice format. Forces estimation and comparison before seeing the answer.

#### compare_pair
Two items shown side by side. Student taps which one is greater/better/correct.

- **Bloom's**: Understand/Evaluate (L2-L5)
- **Time**: 5-10s
- **Mobile**: Tap left or right -- extremely fast
- **Best for**: Math (number comparison), vocabulary, science
- **Example** (place value): "Which is greater?" Left: 3,892 | Right: 3,928 -> tap right.
- **Why**: Lightning fast. Good for interleaving between heavier activities. Binary choice feels decisive.

#### tap_to_eliminate
Show 4-5 options. Student taps to strike through wrong answers one at a time until only correct one remains.

- **Bloom's**: Analyze (L4) -- must evaluate each option against criteria
- **Time**: 10-20s
- **Mobile**: Tap to cross out -- satisfying on mobile
- **Best for**: All subjects. Especially "tricky" questions with plausible distractors.
- **Example** (place value): "Which number has 5 in the tens place? 521, 354, 253, 150." Cross out wrong ones.
- **Why**: Game-show feel. Process of elimination is a real thinking skill. More engaging than just picking the right answer.

### Tier 3 -- Specialized but valuable

#### balance_scale
A visual balance is shown. Student drags items onto one side to make it equal to the other.

- **Bloom's**: Apply/Analyze (L3-L4) -- requires understanding of equivalence
- **Time**: 10-25s
- **Mobile**: Drag items onto scale pans
- **Best for**: Math (equality, equations, place value decomposition)
- **Example** (place value): Left side shows "400". Right side has blocks: [100][100][100][100][10][10]. Student drags four 100-blocks.
- **Why**: Manipulative feel. Strong for equivalence and decomposition concepts specifically.

#### cause_effect_chain
Show 3-4 events. Student connects them in causal order by tapping/dragging.

- **Bloom's**: Analyze (L4) -- understanding causal relationships
- **Time**: 15-30s
- **Mobile**: Tap items in causal order or drag arrows
- **Best for**: Science (processes), history (events), math (reasoning chains)
- **Example** (place value): "Connect: 'Add 10 to ones' -> 'Ones overflow past 9' -> 'Carry 1 to tens' -> 'Tens digit goes up by 1'."
- **Why**: Great for procedural/causal understanding. Makes invisible reasoning chains visible.

#### step_builder
A question that builds across 2-3 quick parts. Answer from part 1 feeds into part 2.

- **Bloom's**: Apply/Analyze (L3-L4) -- tests procedural understanding step by step
- **Time**: 15-30s total
- **Mobile**: Sequential card flow
- **Best for**: Math (multi-step problems), science (processes)
- **Example** (place value): Part 1: "What is the value of 5 in 3,527?" -> 500. Part 2: "If you change that 5 to a 7, how much does the number increase by?" -> 200.
- **Why**: Catches procedural errors. Students can get part 1 right but fail part 2, revealing shallow understanding.

#### highlight_the_evidence
Show a passage or worked solution. Student taps/highlights the specific part that answers a question.

- **Bloom's**: Analyze (L4) -- evidence identification
- **Time**: 10-20s
- **Mobile**: Tap words/phrases to highlight
- **Best for**: Reading comprehension, math (word problems), science
- **Example** (place value): "The school has 1,247 students. Tap the digit that tells how many hundreds." Student taps "2".
- **Why**: Connects reading to mathematical thinking. Forces precision over vague understanding.

#### multi_select
Show 4-5 options. Student selects ALL correct ones (not just one). Number of correct answers unknown.

- **Bloom's**: Analyze (L4) -- each option must be evaluated independently
- **Time**: 10-20s
- **Mobile**: Tap to toggle selections, then confirm
- **Best for**: All subjects. "Which of these are examples of..."
- **Example** (place value): "Select all numbers where 4 is in the hundreds place: 412, 248, 1,432, 4,000, 3,421." Answer: 412 and 1,432.
- **Why**: Harder than pick_one because student can't use elimination. Must evaluate each option on its own merit.

#### confidence_check
After answering any question, follow-up asks "How sure are you?" (guessing / somewhat sure / very sure).

- **Bloom's**: Metacognition (above Bloom's) -- builds self-monitoring
- **Time**: 3-5s (add-on to any question)
- **Mobile**: Tap one of 3 buttons
- **Not standalone**: Attached to other formats
- **Why**: Identifies "confidently wrong" students (most dangerous misconceptions) and "correct but unsure" students (fragile knowledge). No extra points, just awareness.

## Bloom's Taxonomy Coverage

| Bloom's Level | Current Formats | Proposed Additions |
|---|---|---|
| Remember (L1) | pick_one, true_false | -- |
| Understand (L2) | fill_blank, match_pairs | compare_pair |
| Apply (L3) | sequence | estimation_slider, build_the_answer, balance_scale |
| Analyze (L4) | sort_buckets | spot_the_error, odd_one_out, swipe_classify, tap_to_eliminate, cause_effect_chain, highlight_the_evidence, multi_select |
| Evaluate (L5) | -- | two_truths_one_lie, would_you_rather, predict_then_reveal |
| Create (L6) | -- | build_the_answer (partial) |
| Metacognition | -- | confidence_check |

Current gap: nothing at Evaluate (L5) or Create (L6). The proposed formats fill this.

## Implementation Considerations

- **All formats must work from text alone** -- no dependency on visuals (same as current rule)
- **All text follows easy-english principles** -- under 12 words, no idioms, Indian contexts
- **Each format needs**: instruction, hint (one-sentence nudge), success_message (warm + reinforcing)
- **Struggle tracking**: wrong_count, hints_shown, auto_revealed (same as current)
- **Swipe/drag formats** need fallback tap interactions for accessibility
- **estimation_slider** needs defined tolerance range per question for partial credit scoring
- **confidence_check** is an add-on, not a standalone format -- attach to any other format
- **step_builder** is multi-card by nature -- needs special sequencing logic in the card pipeline
