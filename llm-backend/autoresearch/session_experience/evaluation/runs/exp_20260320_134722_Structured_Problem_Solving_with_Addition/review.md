# Session Experience Review

**Topic:** Structured Problem Solving with Addition
**Date:** 2026-03-20 13:51:14
**Naturalness Score:** 5/10
**Issues:** 1 critical, 3 major, 1 minor

---

## Flow Assessment

The card phase is structurally well-intentioned but far too heavy for this student profile — 14 cards covering a 5-step framework, two-part problems, subtraction, and three checking methods is multiple sessions worth of content delivered in one pass, to a student who needs repetition and simplicity. The interactive session then opens with a double question demanding synthesis of everything just read, which is the highest-risk moment for a student who responds to overwhelm with hollow agreement rather than honest confusion.

## Strengths

Cards 1 and 4-5 are genuinely strong — the packing-a-bag analogy and the 'clock on the table' image for extra numbers are concrete, everyday, and memorable for a 10-year-old. The welcome message is warm, brief, and sets an encouraging tone before the card sequence begins.

## Flagged Messages

### 1. Turn Card 7 — premature_advance [MAJOR]

> Now let's solve and check. Step 4 — Solve: 156 + 238. Remember regrouping fro

The phrase 'Remember regrouping from our earlier lessons?' assumes prior context that may not exist. If the student doesn't remember or this is new territory, this creates a quiet anxiety rather than confidence — and this student won't say so.

*Context:* This appears mid-card in a 14-card sequence on a new topic. A student who never admits confusion will just push past the phrase without actually recalling anything, undermining the worked example that follows.

### 2. Turn Cards 9-10 — information_dump [CRITICAL]

> Some problems ask you two things. And here's the tricky part — you need the an

Cards 9-10 introduce two-part problems requiring subtraction — a second problem type — before the student has practiced the core 5-step framework at all. For a student who needs 2-3 passes before anything clicks, stacking a new concept here is a critical overload.

*Context:* The student has just read 8 cards covering the full problem-solving checklist with multiple worked examples. Introducing two-part/subtraction problems before any interactive practice means they arrive at the teaching phase carrying two half-understood frameworks instead of one solid one.

### 3. Turn Cards 11-13 — overwhelming [MAJOR]

> Check 1 — Is My Math Right? After you solve, try adding the numbers in the oth

Three consecutive cards introducing three distinct checking methods land back-to-back after the student has already absorbed a 5-step checklist, two analogies, three worked examples, and a two-part problem. Each check is presented as new content, not a reminder — the cumulative weight is too much.

*Context:* By card 11, this student's working memory is almost certainly saturated. Cards 11-13 arrive as fresh instructional content at exactly the moment they should be wrapping up. Card 13 (checking someone else's work) in particular introduces an entirely new scenario that adds cognitive load without proportional value.

### 4. Turn Card 14 — repetitive_pattern [MINOR]

> Word problems can look scary, but you now have a plan — like a checklist befor

After 13 cards, a full-recap summary restates the checklist, extra numbers, two-part problems, and all three checks again. For a student already losing focus, this reads as another wall of text — nothing new, just more volume.

*Context:* Cards 11-13 just introduced the three checks. Card 14 immediately re-lists all of them plus the earlier material. The ASCII art formatting may also not render clearly depending on the device, making it look cluttered rather than helpful.

### 5. Turn 0 — overwhelming [MAJOR]

> Remember the packing-a-bag idea from the cards? Let's see what stuck: if a wor

The transition into interactive teaching opens with two questions in one message: how to decide which numbers to use AND what to look for first before adding. For a student who just absorbed 14 cards and won't admit confusion, a double synthesis question will produce a surface echo of card language — a textbook false-OK moment.

*Context:* The student has just clicked through 14 dense cards. The first interactive message should ease them in with one concrete, low-stakes question to check a single idea. Instead this message asks them to both identify their decision process AND articulate the first step — which are actually the same question asked twice, adding confusion rather than focus.
