## Explanation Cards

### Card 1 (analogy): Solving Problems is Like Packing a Bag

Imagine you're packing a bag for a picnic. You wouldn't just throw everything in, right?

First, you'd read the list of what to bring. Then you'd pick only the right things. Then you'd check your bag before you leave.

Solving word problems works the same way. There are steps, and if you follow them in order, you won't miss anything.

And just like packing a bag, once you learn the steps, it gets easy!

### Card 2 (concept): Step 1 — Read and Find the Question

Before you do any adding, stop and ask yourself: "What is this problem asking me to find?"

That's the answer you need to find. It's like reading your picnic list before you touch anything in the kitchen.

If you don't know what the question is asking, you can't pick the right numbers to use.

### Card 3 (example): Finding the Question

Let's try one.

"Riya has 24 stickers. Her friend gives her 13 more. How many stickers does Riya have now?"

Before we add anything, let's ask: what does the problem want to know?

It wants the total number of stickers Riya has. That's the answer we need to find. Now we know where we're headed.

### Card 4 (analogy): Watch Out for Extra Numbers

Sometimes a word problem gives you numbers you don't actually need. That's okay — you just need to spot them and skip them.

Think of it this way — if someone puts sandwiches, juice boxes, AND a clock on the table, you wouldn't pack the clock for your picnic! It was just sitting there.

Some numbers in a problem are like that clock. They're just sitting there. They don't go in your math.

### Card 5 (example): Spotting Extra Numbers

"Aman has 45 marbles. He is 7 years old. His sister gives him 32 more marbles. How many marbles does he have now?"

The question asks about marbles. Does Aman's age (7) help us count marbles? No! That's extra — like the clock on the table.

We only need: 45 marbles + 32 more marbles = 77 marbles.

Watch out! If you added ALL the numbers — 45 + 32 + 7 = 84 — that would be wrong. Aman's age has nothing to do with his marbles!

### Card 6 (example): Let's Try a Problem Together — Part 1

"A shop has 156 red balloons and 238 blue balloons. There are 4 workers in the shop. How many balloons are there in all?"

Let's work through this step by step.

Step 1 — Read: The question asks for the total number of balloons.

Step 2 — Find: 156 red, 238 blue. The 4 workers? That's extra — workers aren't balloons!

Step 3 — Pick: We need the total, so we add.

### Card 7 (example): Let's Try a Problem Together — Part 2

Now let's solve and check.

Step 4 — Solve: 156 + 238.
Remember regrouping from our earlier lessons? 6 + 8 = 14, write 4, carry 1. Then 1 + 5 + 3 = 9. Then 1 + 2 = 3. Answer: 394.

Step 5 — Check: Is 394 right? We had over 100 and over 200, so close to 400 sounds right. Did we answer the question? Yes — total balloons. Our math checks out!

### Card 8 (visual): Your Problem-Solving Checklist

You just used these steps to solve the balloon problem! Here they are written out so you can follow them every time.

### Card 9 (analogy): Two-Part Problems — Build on What You Found

Some problems ask you two things. And here's the tricky part — you need the answer from the first part to solve the second part.

It's like building with blocks. You build the first stack, and then that stack becomes the bottom of the next one. You don't start over — you build on top.

Your first answer works the same way. It becomes the starting number for the next part.

### Card 10 (example): A Two-Part Problem

"Priya picked 125 mangoes on Monday and 248 mangoes on Tuesday. How many did she pick in all? If she gives 100 mangoes to her neighbour, how many does she have left?"

Part 1: Total mangoes = 125 + 248 = 373.

Part 2 uses subtraction — taking away — which you already know. Take the answer from Part 1: 373 - 100 = 273 left.

See how 373 from Part 1 became the starting number for Part 2? That's building on what you found.

### Card 11 (concept): Check 1 — Is My Math Right?

After you solve, try adding the numbers in the other order. If you get the same answer, your math is right!

You learned this before — swapping the order doesn't change the answer.

So if you did 125 + 248 = 373, try 248 + 125. Did you get 373 again? Great — your adding is correct!

### Card 12 (concept): Checks 2 and 3 — Complete and Makes Sense?

Check 2 — Did I answer everything? Read the question again. If it asked two things, did you give two answers? Don't stop halfway.

Check 3 — Does my answer make sense? If someone had 50 apples and got 30 more, and you got 800 — that can't be right! The answer should feel right for the story.

### Card 13 (example): Checking Someone Else's Work

"A library has 245 English books and 368 Hindi books. How many books in all?" Rohan wrote: 503. Is he right?

Don't just look at Rohan's answer and think "that looks okay." Do the math yourself.

245 + 368: 5 + 8 = 13 (write 3, carry 1). 1 + 4 + 6 = 11 (write 1, carry 1). 1 + 2 + 3 = 6. That gives us 613.

Rohan wrote 503. That's wrong — the correct answer is 613. He missed a carry. Always work it out yourself!

### Card 14 (summary): You've Got This

Word problems can look scary, but you now have a plan — like a checklist before a trip.

Read the problem. Find what matters — and skip extra numbers. Pick add or subtract. Solve carefully — you already know how to add big numbers with regrouping. Then check three ways — is the math right, did I answer everything, and does it make sense.

For two-part problems, build on what you found — use your first answer in the next step.

Remember — not every number in the problem goes into your math. Read the question first!

You don't have to do it all in your head. Just follow the steps, one at a time.
