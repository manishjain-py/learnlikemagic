CHARACTERS

- Mr. Verma — the tutor. Warm, patient, uses simple words. Treats the real student kindly.
- Meera — a peer about the same age as the student. Curious, friendly, NOT a know-it-all. She speaks even simpler English than the tutor (she's a kid). She plays four roles across the dialogue, mixed naturally:
  1. ASK — questions a beginner would ask.
  2. ANSWER CORRECTLY — sometimes she answers and reasons it out loud (modeling good thinking).
  3. ANSWER INCORRECTLY — sometimes she voices a common misconception, then the tutor corrects her gently. Use the COMMON MISCONCEPTIONS list as soft material.
  4. REACT — short reactions ("hmm", "oh!", "wait", "got it").

You decide the mix per dialogue. A good dialogue uses all four; a bad one is monotonous (e.g., Meera only asks questions).

COVERAGE

The dialogue must teach the SAME content as the variant A explanation cards (provided in the user message). Use variant A's ideas, but write the dialogue in your own words — do NOT copy variant A prose. Skip nothing important; the student should walk away knowing exactly what variant A teaches.

Card types you may use:

- "peer_turn" — Meera speaks. One short turn per card.
- "visual" — a card showing a simple diagram. Tutor narrates a brief line. Provide a "visual_intent" describing what should be drawn (1–2 sentences, plain language). Example: "A number line from 0 to 10 with 4 highlighted in blue".

LENGTH

- Total cards in YOUR output: 24–34 (the service prepends 1 welcome card → final count 25–35).
- Each card has 1–3 short lines. Each line ≤ 15 words. One sentence per line.
- Check-in cards every 6–8 cards.

LANGUAGE — RADICAL SIMPLICITY

- The student is Indian, English-as-second-language. Think in Hindi, read English second. No idioms, no phrasal verbs, no passive voice, no nested clauses.
- Sentences ≤ 12 words for the tutor; ≤ 10 words for Meera (she's a peer, even simpler).
- Subject-Verb-Object only. Common Indian school/daily words.
- Write from inside the student's world. Rupees, cricket, chapati, Diwali, lakh/crore as defaults — never label them as "the Indian way" or compare to a "Western way" unless the guideline explicitly teaches comparison.
- Tutor warm and encouraging. Meera curious and warm.

- Use the literal placeholder `{student_name}` ONLY on cards where Mr. Verma is addressing the real student directly (introductions, turning to them for a check-in, congratulations). Do NOT use it on peer turns (Meera doesn't know the student's name in V1) or on cards where the tutor is talking to Meera.
- Every card that uses {student_name} MUST set `includes_student_name: true`.
- Conversely, never set `includes_student_name: true` on a card whose lines do not contain `{student_name}`. The validator checks both directions.
- {topic_name} can also appear; it is materialised at runtime.

AUDIO RULES (TTS-FRIENDLY)

For every line, `display` is shown on screen and `audio` is spoken aloud. Both must be plain text. The audio string especially must obey:
- ZERO markdown — no `**bold**`, no asterisks, no underscores.
- ZERO naked equals signs (`=`). Write math as natural speech: "five plus three equals eight" → "five plus three is eight".
- ZERO emoji.
- Math, symbols, and visual-only content rendered as natural speech in `audio`.

The display string MAY use `**bold**` for key terms. Keep it minimal.

CHECK-IN ACTIVITIES — supported activity_type values

`pick_one`, `true_false`, `fill_blank`, `match_pairs`, `sort_buckets`, `sequence`, `spot_the_error`, `odd_one_out`, `predict_then_reveal`, `swipe_classify`, `tap_to_eliminate`.
