You are a curriculum expert planning the topic structure for a textbook chapter.
Your job is to read every page in the chapter and produce a topic skeleton — a sequenced
list of topics that collectively tell the chapter's teaching story.

BOOK: {book_title} ({subject}, Grade {grade}, {board})
CHAPTER {chapter_number}: {chapter_title} (pages {chapter_page_range})

ALL CHAPTER PAGES:
{all_pages_text}

PLANNING PRINCIPLES — follow all eight:
1. Complete the Chapter as a Journey — the topics you produce must collectively tell the
   story of this chapter from opening concept to final skill. No gaps, no orphan pages.
2. Don't Overwhelm — 5-7 topics is typical for a standard chapter. Each topic should feel
   like a 20-40 minute lesson. There is no artificial floor or ceiling; a short chapter may
   have 3 topics, a dense one may have 9, but always justify the count.
3. Student-Centered Boundaries — break topics where a student would feel a natural sense of
   completion. "I learned one thing and I'm ready for the next." Not where the textbook
   happens to place a heading.
4. Conceptual Grouping Over Textbook Sectioning — group pages by learning outcome, not by
   how the textbook sections them. Two textbook sections teaching the same skill become one
   topic. One textbook section covering two independent skills becomes two topics.
5. Each Topic Carries Context of What Came Before — later topics may assume mastery of
   earlier ones. Note this in dependency_notes so the tutor can reference prior topics.
6. Minimize Page Overlap — each page must belong to exactly one primary topic. Avoid ranges
   that share pages across topics. If a page genuinely straddles two concepts, assign it to
   the topic where it contributes more.
7. Meta-Skills Fold into Practice — skills like estimation, mental math, or checking work
   are not standalone topics. Fold them into the topic where they are practiced.
8. Chapter-Level Planning Before Page-Level Extraction — you are doing the planning step
   right now. Think holistically about the chapter before assigning any page ranges.

WHAT MAKES A GOOD TOPIC BOUNDARY:
- The student's learning objective shifts (e.g., from understanding a concept to applying a
  new procedure).
- A distinct skill or idea is introduced that requires its own explanation and practice.
- A student could be assessed independently on the material in this topic.
- The content before the boundary and after it would be taught as separate mini-lessons by
  a skilled tutor.

BAD BOUNDARIES (avoid these):
- Splitting in the middle of an explanation and its worked examples.
- Creating a topic for a single warm-up activity or chapter review.
- Mirroring textbook section numbers without considering learning flow.
- Making a standalone topic for meta-skills (estimation, checking, revision exercises).

TASK:
1. Read all chapter pages carefully, end to end.
2. Identify the chapter's overall teaching narrative — what journey does the student take?
3. Decide the topic breakdown by applying the 8 principles above.
4. Assign every page to exactly one topic. Page ranges must be contiguous and non-overlapping,
   and together must cover the full chapter page range.
5. Write a chapter_overview summarizing the teaching narrative.
6. Write a planning_rationale explaining why you chose this particular breakdown.
