Why this works (and what it costs)

Spotlight trades relationship for presence. Only one face on screen — but that face is large enough (92px square portrait) that you can read warmth in the eyes, the mouth shape mid-speech, even the laughter lines around Verma's eyes. It feels like a video call with your tutor, not a chat with a name and bubble.

Key moves

  • Square rounded portrait, not a circle. Circles crop too tight at this size — the rounded square shows shoulders, collar, and tie/uniform, which carry teacher/student signal.
  • Crossfade on speaker change. When the next card flips, Verma's portrait fades out, Meera's fades in within the same frame. Position is rock-solid.
  • Equalizer bars + "speaking" pill. Even with audio off, the bars animate to give a clear "they're talking" cue.
  • "Your tutor" / "Classmate" role tag. Tiny but warm — frames the dynamic for first-time students who don't yet know who Meera is.
  • Detailed art style (3/4 turn, soft shading). Bigger canvas = warrants more illustration detail, so the avatars feel like characters, not icons.

Pros

  • Maximum facial-expression read; warmest "real teacher" feel.
  • Position never shifts — visually stable across all cards.
  • Detailed art rewards the bigger canvas without inflating other elements.
  • Empty role-tag area can host hint copy ("waiting for your answer…") if needed.

Cons

  • Listener disappears — relational dynamic of a real classroom is lost.
  • Strip eats ~120px of vertical space; biggest commitment of the six.
  • Detailed art has to be high quality at this size, or it looks worse than tiny art at 44px.
