Why this works (and what it costs)

Every other variant adds graphics on top of the chalkboard. This one says: the chalkboard is the surface, and the avatars are drawn on it. White line-art with chalky softness, handwritten names in Caveat, a chalk-arrow pointing from the listener's portrait to the speaker's. The listener is faded like a half-erased sketch.

Key moves

  • Pure line-art, no fills. Reads as "drawn on the board" instead of "stuck onto the board."
  • Active speaker has fuller strokes + a "↓ talking" handwritten cue.
  • Inactive speaker is half-erased. Lower opacity, slightly blurred, broken-line shoulders. Feels like a chalk drawing in mid-fade.
  • Speech dots float from the active mouth. Tiny chalk dots leading toward the listener — a sketchy speech-trail.
  • Handwritten names in Caveat font. Continues the "teacher wrote this" feel.
  • No card frame around portraits. They live directly on the chalkboard surface, sharing texture with everything else.

Pros

  • Most cohesive with the existing chalkboard theme — feels native, not bolted on.
  • Cheapest art to produce and iterate (line drawings, no shading, no skin tones to manage).
  • Won't compete with the visual aid card or color illustrations — chalk-white is monochromatic.
  • Has the most personality of the six — feels like the teacher's hand drew the entire app.

Cons

  • Monochrome = less of a "real teacher" face cue. May feel less warm than the colored options.
  • Can come across as low-fidelity / amateur if the line work isn't carefully polished.
  • Hard to convey age, ethnicity, or warmth at small sizes with line art alone.
  • Limits future expressiveness — adding color or photos later would clash with the established style.
