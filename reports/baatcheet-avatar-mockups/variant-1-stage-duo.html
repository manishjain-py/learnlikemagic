Why this works (and what it costs)

The current chip is a single 44px circle that hides the listener. Cognitively, students see "one face talking" instead of "two people in conversation." Stage Duo restores the relationship by always rendering both — and uses size, color saturation, and a soft pulse to make the speaker unmistakable without visual noise.

Key moves

  • Both avatars persistent. Verma always on the left, Meera always on the right — positions never swap, so the eye always knows where to look.
  • Active is bigger + saturated. 60px gold-ringed for active, 38px desaturated/dimmed for listener. Color and size carry the same message redundantly.
  • They face each other. Meera is mirrored so her gaze runs toward Verma. Two profiles in conversation feels alive; two front-facing portraits feels like a yearbook.
  • Single name label, gold pulse. Only the active speaker's name appears below the row, anchored to keep the header height stable.
  • Tiny "▶" cue on the speaker. Reinforces talking state for the audio-off case where there's no pulse animation distinction.

Pros

  • Strongest "real conversation" feel of any variant — eyes track between two characters.
  • Stable header height; only saturation + scale animate.
  • Avatar style (chunky illustrated, Indian-coded features) reads warmly even at 38px.
  • Works for accessibility — color, size, and label all communicate "active speaker."

Cons

  • Eats ~80px of vertical real estate vs. 44px for a single chip.
  • Two avatars on screen drag attention slightly away from the visual aid card.
  • Scaling animation may feel busy after dozens of cards — could mute to opacity-only on a setting.
