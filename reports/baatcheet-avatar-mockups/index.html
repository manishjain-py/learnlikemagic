Mr. Verma & Meera — how should they look?

Six different treatments for the dialogue avatars in Baatcheet, paired with the layouts they enable. Each is rendered on a real iPhone-sized phone frame using the same dialogue moment so you can compare apples to apples. Open any variant for two side-by-side states (Mr. Verma speaking → Meera speaking) plus pros/cons.

The brief, in one paragraph

Today's avatars are 44px chalk-on-circle SVGs. They identify the speaker but feel like placeholders, not people. Goal: make Verma and Meera read as a real teacher and a real student — within tight phone real estate, on top of the chalkboard aesthetic, without crowding out the visual aid that does most of the pedagogical work.

Today's baseline (for comparison)

Single 44px circle, chalk-gold ring when the speaker's audio plays. Chip in the top-left of each card with the speaker's name beside it. The listener is invisible.

How to choose between them

  • Want both characters always present? V1 (Stage Duo), V4 (Polaroid), V5 (Classroom Scene), and V6 (Chalk) all keep the listener in frame. V2 and V3 only show the active speaker (V3 fades the prior turn for context).
  • Tightest on vertical space? V3 (Chat Bubbles) is by far most efficient — avatar lives inline. V1 is moderate. V4 and V5 are heaviest.
  • Want it to feel most "real teacher"? V2 (Spotlight) has the strongest face read; V4 (Polaroid) has the strongest "physical artifact" cue.
  • Want it to feel most kid-friendly / most familiar? V3 (Chat Bubbles) leverages WhatsApp; V5 (Classroom Scene) leverages storybook illustration.
  • Want it to feel most theme-cohesive? V6 (Chalk Portraits) is born of the chalkboard — feels native, not bolted on.
  • Worried about build effort? V1, V3, and V6 are the cheapest — line art or simple flat shapes. V2 needs a careful illustration. V4 and V5 need bespoke art that holds up at small sizes.
  • Worth combining? V1 + V6 chalk-style is feasible — keep the duo layout but swap to chalk portraits. V2 + V5 — spotlight inside a scene background — also possible.

Notes on the SVG art in these mockups

Every avatar in these files is hand-coded SVG, drawn to demo each style. They're not final art — if we ship one of these directions, we'd commission an illustrator (or generate + iterate with Midjourney / Stable Diffusion) to lock the character design. The SVG here is enough to show the style direction, not the final faces.

Same goes for the dialogue and visuals — every variant uses the same dialogue moment ("…how would you read 47,352 out loud?" → "forty-seven thousand three hundred fifty-two") so you're comparing avatar treatment, not content.
